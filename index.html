Train a biased model. Measure the fairness gap. Engineer a fair model. Measure again. No theory. Just data, code, and results.

EXPLAINER — 01

WHAT IS A PROXY VARIABLE?

The sneaky reason AI stays biased even after you remove race from the data.

One-Sentence Definition

A proxy variable is a data feature that correlates with a protected attribute — like race, gender, or class — even though it doesn't mention it directly. So removing the protected attribute doesn't remove the bias it encodes.

Why This Matters

Most people assume that removing race from an AI model makes it race-neutral. This is wrong. Machine learning models don't care what a feature is called. They care what it predicts. If a feature like custody_status or zip_code is correlated with race in the training data, the model will learn and use that correlation — whether or not the word "race" appears anywhere in the dataset. This is called proxy discrimination, and it's one of the hardest problems in algorithmic fairness.

Common Proxy Variables
Feature Encodes Why
zip_code Race Historical redlining segregated neighbourhoods. Zip codes still reflect this.
custody_status Race Over-policing of Black communities leads to disproportionate pretrial detention.
name Gender / Ethnicity Names encode gender and ethnicity with high accuracy.
college_attended Socioeconomic class Elite colleges remain heavily stratified by class and race.
prior_arrests Race Arrest records reflect over-policing, not actual crime rates.
employment_tenure Age A 24-year-old structurally cannot have 10 years of work history.

How to Detect Proxy Variables
detect_proxy.py
from scipy.stats import chi2_contingency

def check_proxy(df, feature, protected_attr):
  """Returns p-value. If < 0.05, likely a proxy."""
  contingency = pd.crosstab(df[feature], df[protected_attr])
  chi2, p, dof, _ = chi2_contingency(contingency)
  return { 'p_value': round(p, 4), 'is_proxy': p < 0.05 }

# Run on every feature before training
result = check_proxy(df, 'CustodyStatus', 'race')
# → { 'p_value': 0.0001, 'is_proxy': True }
The Bigger Picture

Proxy variables exist because our social world is stratified. Zip codes encode race because of redlining. Custody records encode race because of over-policing. Employment tenure encodes age because time is linear. Data doesn't exist in a vacuum — it reflects the society that generated it. A model trained on that data will learn those reflections, unless you actively audit and intervene.

Algorithmic auditing is not optional. It is a prerequisite for deployment.

EXPLAINER — 02

WHAT IS SAMPLING BIAS?

The reason your AI works great in the lab and fails on the people who need it most.

One-Sentence Definition

Sampling bias occurs when the data used to train an AI system doesn't accurately represent the population it will be deployed on — so the model learns patterns that don't generalise, performing well for over-represented groups and failing for under-represented ones.

Why This Matters

Most people assume that if you have a large dataset, the model will be fair. This is wrong. Size doesn't equal representativeness. A dataset of 10 million records is still biased if 9.8 million of them come from the same demographic. The model doesn't know what it hasn't seen — it optimises for the distribution it was trained on. When that distribution doesn't match the real world, the people left out pay the price. This is how facial recognition fails on darker skin tones, clinical tools miss diagnoses in women, and NLP models perform worse on dialects outside Standard American English.

Common Forms of Sampling Bias
Form What It Means Real Example
Underrepresentation A group appears far less in training data than in the real world Facial recognition trained mostly on light-skinned faces
Selection Bias Data collected from a non-random subset of the population Clinical AI trained on records from affluent ZIP codes only
Historical Bias Past data reflects past inequities; the model learns the inequity Hiring models trained on 10 years of male-dominated engineering hires
Survivorship Bias Only "successful" cases make it into the dataset; failures are invisible Loan models trained only on approved applicants, never denied ones
Measurement Bias The same reality is measured differently for different groups Over-policing creates more arrest records for Black communities — not higher crime
Real-World Proof: MIT Gender Shades (2018)

Joy Buolamwini & Timnit Gebru tested three commercial facial recognition systems on a dataset balanced across gender and skin tone. Every system performed dramatically worse on darker-skinned women — not because the algorithms were programmed to fail them, but because training datasets were overwhelmingly composed of lighter-skinned faces. The fix was not a new algorithm. It was a more representative dataset.

Group Best System Error Rate Worst System Error Rate
Lighter-skinned males ~0.8% ~3.4%
Lighter-skinned females ~1.7% ~7.1%
Darker-skinned males ~10.8% ~16.0%
Darker-skinned females ~20.8% ~34.7%
A 34-percentage-point gap — from the same model, on the same task, because of who was and wasn't in the training data.

How to Detect Sampling Bias
audit_representation.py
def audit_representation(df, group_col, expected_proportions):
  """Flag groups as OVER/UNDERREPRESENTED vs real-world."""
  proportions = df[group_col].value_counts(normalize=True)
  audit = pd.DataFrame({'dataset_proportion': proportions})
  audit['expected'] = pd.Series(expected_proportions)
  audit['ratio'] = (audit['dataset_proportion'] / audit['expected']).round(4)
  audit['flag'] = audit['ratio'].apply(
    lambda r: 'OVERREPRESENTED' if r > 1.5
              else 'UNDERREPRESENTED' if r < 0.67
              else 'OK'
  )
  return audit

# Run before every training job
real_world = {'men': 0.51, 'women': 0.49}
result = audit_representation(df, 'gender', real_world)
# UNDERREPRESENTED → oversample, reweight, or restrict deployment
The Bigger Picture

Sampling bias is not a technical accident. It reflects who had access to the systems that generated the data in the first place. Facial recognition datasets skew lighter-skinned because the researchers who built them moved in predominantly lighter-skinned professional environments. Clinical AI skews wealthy because hospitals in affluent ZIP codes have better electronic health record infrastructure. NLP models skew toward Standard American English because that's what most of the training corpus is written in. The data you have is a record of the world as it was — including which people were visible, documented, and deemed worth measuring. A model trained on that data will reproduce those decisions at scale.

You cannot fix a representativeness problem with a better algorithm. You fix it with better data — or you disclose the limitation and restrict deployment accordingly.

WHAT ARE SHAP VALUES?

The reason "the model said so" is not an explanation — and what to do instead.

One-Sentence Definition

SHAP values (SHapley Additive exPlanations) tell you exactly how much each feature contributed to a specific model prediction — in a unit you can reason about — so you can see what actually drove a decision, not just what the decision was.

Why This Matters

Most AI systems deployed in high-stakes domains — criminal justice, hiring, lending, healthcare — are black boxes. They produce a score or a decision and don't explain why. This is not just an inconvenience. It is a fairness problem. If you cannot inspect how a model arrived at a decision, you cannot detect whether a protected attribute drove the outcome, challenge a decision that affected you, or comply with regulations that require explainability. SHAP values make the black box transparent. They are grounded in cooperative game theory (Shapley values), providing a mathematically principled way to attribute credit or blame across features — consistent, locally accurate, and model-agnostic.

SHAP vs Other Explainability Methods
Method Scope Consistent?
SHAP Local + Global ✓ Mathematically guaranteed — values sum exactly to the prediction
LIME Local only ✗ Approximate — results can vary between runs
Feature Importance Global only ✗ Biased toward high-cardinality features; no per-decision insight
Permutation Importance Global only Reasonable, but tells you nothing about individual decisions

How to Run a SHAP Bias Audit
shap_bias_audit.py
import shap
import pandas as pd

# 1. Fit explainer (fast for tree models)
explainer = shap.TreeExplainer(model)
shap_values = explainer.shap_values(X_test)

def shap_bias_audit(shap_vals, feature_names, protected):
  """Flag protected attributes / proxies in top drivers."""
  mean_abs = pd.Series(shap_vals.mean(axis=0), index=feature_names).abs()
  mean_abs = mean_abs.sort_values(ascending=False).reset_index()
  mean_abs.columns = ['feature', 'mean_abs_shap']
  mean_abs['flag'] = mean_abs['feature'].apply(
    lambda f: 'BIAS RISK' if any(p in f for p in protected) else '')
  return mean_abs

# Run before deployment — every time
protected = ['race', 'gender', 'age', 'CustodyStatus']
audit = shap_bias_audit(shap_values[1], X_test.columns, protected)
# → Rows flagged BIAS RISK → remove from feature set
The Bigger Picture

Explainability is not a nice-to-have. It is the mechanism by which accountability becomes possible. A person denied a loan, flagged as high-risk in a courtroom, or rejected by a hiring algorithm has a legitimate interest in knowing why. SHAP values do not fix bias — removing protected attributes and proxy variables fixes bias. What SHAP does is make the model's reasoning visible, so you can catch problems before deployment, explain decisions to the people affected, and demonstrate to auditors and regulators that the system is operating as intended. The pipeline is not complete until you can explain every decision the model makes.

Accuracy tells you how often the model is right. SHAP tells you why — and that's where the bias hides.

WHAT IS EQUALIZED ODDS?

The fairness metric that catches a model treating two groups differently — even when overall accuracy looks fine.

One-Sentence Definition

Equalized Odds requires a model to make equally accurate decisions for every demographic group — meaning its true positive rate and false positive rate must be the same across protected groups like race, gender, or age.

Why This Matters

Most people check model accuracy and call it done. This misses the problem entirely. A model can be 90% accurate overall while still making systematically different types of mistakes for different groups. It might correctly identify qualified candidates in one group at 91%, while only correctly identifying them in another group at 69%. Same model. Same task. Different outcomes — depending on who you are.

This matters because the two types of errors a model makes are not morally equivalent. False negatives — missing someone who should have been flagged — can mean a qualified person is rejected, a sick patient goes undiagnosed, or a creditworthy borrower is denied a loan. False positives — flagging someone who shouldn't have been — can mean an innocent person is detained, or a healthy patient is over-treated. If those errors fall unevenly across demographic groups, the model is discriminating, regardless of what its accuracy score says.

Real-World Proof: COMPAS Analysis

COMPAS doesn't just need to be accurate — it needs to be equally accurate for every defendant, regardless of race. Measuring Equalized Odds reveals what aggregate accuracy hides.

Group True Positive Rate False Positive Rate
Black Defendants 0.89 0.31 ← flagged when innocent
White Defendants 0.67 0.11
Gap 0.22 0.20 — nearly 3× the rate
Black defendants who won't reoffend are falsely flagged as high-risk at nearly three times the rate of white defendants. Aggregate accuracy tells you none of this. Equalized Odds does.

The Math

Two conditions must hold simultaneously. Equal True Positive Rate: P(Ŷ=1 | Y=1, A=a) = P(Ŷ=1 | Y=1, A=b). And Equal False Positive Rate: P(Ŷ=1 | Y=0, A=a) = P(Ŷ=1 | Y=0, A=b). Where Ŷ is the model prediction, Y is the true label, and A is the protected attribute. A model that satisfies one condition but not the other still violates Equalized Odds.

Related Fairness Metrics
Metric What It Requires Trade-off
Equalized Odds Equal TPR + equal FPR across groups May reduce overall accuracy; conflicts with calibration when base rates differ
Equal Opportunity Equal TPR only Relaxed version — lets FPR disparities go unchecked

The Bigger Picture

Equalized Odds violations exist because our models are trained on data generated by unequal systems. A criminal justice dataset carries decades of over-policing. WHAT IS DISPARATE IMPACT (THE 80% RULE)?

The legal test that has decided employment discrimination cases since 1971 — and the simplest fairness check you can run on any hiring model.

One-Sentence Definition

Disparate Impact is a legal and statistical test that flags a selection process as discriminatory when the selection rate for a protected group is less than 80% of the selection rate for the most-selected group. That threshold is the Four-Fifths Rule, codified in the EEOC's Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures (1978), 29 CFR §1607.4(D).

Why This Matters

Most fairness metrics are abstract. The 80% rule is not. It is the actual threshold the EEOC, federal courts, and the Department of Labor use to decide whether to investigate an employer for discriminatory hiring, promotion, or firing. A model can have 95% accuracy and still fail the 80% rule — accuracy says nothing about who is being filtered out at the gate.

This is the rule that matters in practice: HR teams use it to screen vendor hiring tools before deployment. Auditors use it to flag protected-class disparities. Plaintiffs use it as prima facie evidence in Title VII lawsuits. And procurement teams put it in vendor contracts for AI screening products.

disparate_impact.py
import pandas as pd

def disparate_impact_ratio(df, prediction_col, group_col, positive_label=1):
  """Four-Fifths Rule: (lowest selection rate) / (highest selection rate)."""
  selection_rates = (
    df.assign(_selected=(df[prediction_col] == positive_label).astype(int))
      .groupby(group_col)["_selected"]
      .mean()
  )
  ratio = selection_rates.min() / selection_rates.max()
  passes = ratio >= 0.80
  return {
    "selection_rates": selection_rates.to_dict(),
    "disparate_impact_ratio": round(ratio, 3),
    "passes_four_fifths_rule": bool(passes),
  }

The Math

For a protected attribute A taking values a (disadvantaged) and b (advantaged), the selection rate for group g is P(Ŷ=1 | A=g). The Disparate Impact Ratio is DI = P(Ŷ=1 | A=a) / P(Ŷ=1 | A=b), where a is the group with the lower selection rate. The Four-Fifths Rule: DI ≥ 0.80 → no flag; DI < 0.80 → legal scrutiny applies. The ratio always sits in [0, 1] — you always divide the smaller rate by the larger.

WHY FAIRNESS METRICS CONFLICT

You can't be fair to everyone at once — and the math proves it. Any choice of fairness metric is also a choice about who bears the cost of being wrong.

One-Sentence Definition

Fairness metric conflict is the mathematically proven impossibility of satisfying multiple fairness criteria simultaneously when base rates differ across demographic groups — meaning any choice of fairness metric is also a choice about who bears the cost of being wrong.

Why This Matters

Most people assume that making an AI "fair" is a single, solvable problem. It isn't. Researchers have formally proven — not just observed, but proven — that several common fairness definitions are mutually incompatible except in degenerate edge cases.

Courts, regulators, and hiring committees each invoke different fairness definitions — and they are often in direct mathematical conflict. A model optimised to satisfy one metric will necessarily perform worse on another. Choosing a metric is an ethical and political decision, not a technical one. If you deploy a model and declare it "fair" without specifying which metric you used and why, you have not solved the fairness problem. You have hidden it.

Demographic Parity P(Ŷ=1 | Group=A) = P(Ŷ=1 | Group=B) — equal positive outcome rates Hiring, lending, admissions audits. Underpins the 80% rule.
Equalized Odds Equal TPR and equal FPR across groups — equal error rates in both directions Criminal risk assessment, medical screening. ProPublica's COMPAS critique.
Predictive Parity P(Y=1 | Ŷ=1, Group=A) = P(Y=1 | Ŷ=1, Group=B) — equal accuracy of flags Credit scoring, recidivism prediction. Northpointe's defence of COMPAS.
Chouldechova (2017) and Kleinberg et al. (2016) independently proved: when base rates differ between groups, you cannot simultaneously satisfy equalized odds and predictive parity — except when the model is perfect or predicts at random.

def full_fairness_audit(y_true, y_pred, groups):
  """TPR, FPR, and PPV per group — run before deployment."""
  results = {}
  for group in groups.unique():
    mask = groups == group
    tn, fp, fn, tp = confusion_matrix(y_true[mask], y_pred[mask]).ravel()

      'PPV': round(tp / (tp + fp), 3) if (tp + fp) > 0 else None,

WHAT IS CALIBRATION?

The reason a model can be "equally accurate" for everyone — and still treat them unequally.

One-Sentence Definition

A model is calibrated for a group if its predicted probabilities actually match real-world outcomes — so when it says "70% risk," roughly 70% of people in that group actually experience the outcome.

Why This Matters

Most people evaluate AI models on accuracy. Calibration is a different question: do the scores mean the same thing across demographic groups? If a risk-scoring system assigns a "70% recidivism risk" to Black defendants and a "70% risk" to White defendants, calibration asks: does 70% actually mean 70% for both groups? If the model is miscalibrated for one group — say, 70% translates to 50% actual recidivism for White defendants but 80% for Black defendants — then identical scores are producing fundamentally different real-world stakes depending on who receives them.

This is called differential calibration, and it's one of the central problems exposed by the ProPublica COMPAS investigation. It's also why calibration is often in direct conflict with other fairness metrics like equalized odds — you frequently cannot satisfy both at the same time.

How to Measure Calibration

Calibration is measured by comparing predicted probabilities to actual outcome rates within prediction bins. The standard approach is a calibration curve (also called a reliability diagram). A perfectly calibrated model produces a diagonal line from (0,0) to (1,1). Divergence between groups is the differential calibration.

Limitations and Trade-offs

Calibration is not a complete picture of fairness. A model can be calibrated across groups while still generating far more false positives for one group than another — Northpointe's defence of COMPAS is the canonical example. Calibration and equalised false positive/false negative rates are mathematically incompatible when base rates differ between groups. This is the Chouldechova (2017) impossibility result: you have to choose.

Calibration is also sensitive to base rate differences, says nothing about whether the outcome itself is fair (a model calibrated on arrest rates is calibrated on a measure that reflects over-policing, not actual crime), and small group sizes inflate calibration error estimates. Always check sample size per bin before drawing conclusions about a subgroup.

COMPAS chose calibration. ProPublica measured the error rates. Both were right about what they measured. The choice of metric is a choice about who bears the cost of being wrong.

WHAT IS DEMOGRAPHIC PARITY?

Equal outcomes, not equal treatment — and that distinction is everything.

One-Sentence Definition

Demographic parity requires a model to produce positive predictions at equal rates across demographic groups — regardless of whether those groups have different underlying rates of the outcome being predicted.

Why This Matters

Demographic parity is the most widely used fairness metric in practice. It underlies US employment discrimination law (the EEOC's 80% rule), EU AI Act high-risk system audits, and most corporate fairness dashboards. When someone says an algorithm is "biased," they are usually — whether they know it or not — pointing at a demographic parity violation.

A model can be highly accurate overall and still produce systematically unequal outcomes for different groups — demographic parity is the only metric that directly measures this. It is also the legal threshold in hiring, lending, and admissions in most jurisdictions — violating it exposes organisations to disparate impact liability.

The Formal Definition
Form Expression What It Means
Perfect parity P(Ŷ=1 | Group=A) = P(Ŷ=1 | Group=B) Positive prediction rate must be exactly equal across groups
Four-Fifths Rule (EEOC) P(Ŷ=1 | B) / P(Ŷ=1 | A) ≥ 0.80 Legal threshold — a ratio below 0.80 triggers adverse impact scrutiny
The metric makes no claim about whether the predictions are correct — only that the rate of positive predictions is equalised. The 80% threshold is a legal convention set in 1978, not a mathematical optimum. Treat it as a trigger for scrutiny, not a verdict.

import pandas as pd

def demographic_parity_audit(df, prediction_col, group_col, positive_label=1):
  """Compute positive prediction rate per group and the parity gap."""
  rates = (
    df.groupby(group_col)[prediction_col]
      .apply(lambda x: (x == positive_label).mean())
      .round(4).rename('positive_rate')
      .reset_index().sort_values('positive_rate', ascending=False)
  )
  max_rate = rates['positive_rate'].max()
  min_rate = rates['positive_rate'].min()
  gap = round(max_rate - min_rate, 4)
  ratio = round(min_rate / max_rate, 4) if max_rate > 0 else None
  status = '✓ passes' if ratio >= 0.8 else '✗ fails'
  print(f"Parity gap: {gap:.4f} — Four-fifths ratio: {ratio:.4f} {status}")
  return rates, gap, ratio

The Bigger Picture

Demographic parity ignores the ground truth. This is the core tension between parity-based and accuracy-based fairness, and it has no clean technical resolution.

Demographic parity is the legal and practical standard for bias detection in high-stakes systems. Run it first. But it is a floor, not a ceiling — pass it, then audit error rates, calibration, and proxies too.

WHAT IS FEEDBACK LOOP BIAS?

The reason AI systems don't just reflect the past — they actively manufacture more of it.

One-Sentence Definition

Feedback loop bias occurs when a model's predictions influence the real-world outcomes it will later be trained on — so its existing biases are continuously confirmed and amplified across successive retraining cycles, even without anyone introducing new discrimination.

Why This Matters

Proxy variables and sampling bias are problems you can fix at training time: audit your features, remove the proxies, retrain. Feedback loop bias is different — it's a temporal problem. The model doesn't just learn from biased historical data. It generates biased present-day data that becomes the training set for the next version of itself.

This is sometimes called performative prediction: the model's output changes the world, and the changed world feeds back into the model. Left unchecked, this process can take a modestly biased model and turn it into a severely biased one — not because anyone made it worse, but because nothing stopped it from compounding. The mechanism is self-sealing: the model predicts high risk for Group A → Group A receives more scrutiny → more violations are detected in Group A → the next model sees even more Group A violations → it predicts even higher risk for Group A.

Common Feedback Loop Scenarios
Domain Protected Attribute How the Loop Forms
Predictive Policing Race Model flags high-crime zip codes → more patrols deployed → more arrests recorded → next model treats those zip codes as even higher risk
Credit Scoring Race / Class Model denies loans to certain groups → those groups can't build credit history → next model sees thin files and rates them high risk
Hiring AI Gender / Race Model deprioritises candidates from certain schools → those schools produce fewer hires → next model has no positive signal from them
Healthcare Allocation Race Model predicts Group A has lower costs → Group A gets fewer resources → lower recorded utilisation → next model interprets this as lower need
Real-World Proof: Predictive Policing

The most documented case is predictive policing. Systems like PredPol (now Geolitica) train on historical arrest data to predict "high risk" areas. Police are deployed based on those predictions. The flaw is structural: arrest data doesn't measure crime — it measures policing. More patrols generate more arrests, not because more crime happens, but because more enforcement is present. When that arrest data trains the next model, those over-policed areas appear even more dangerous.

  """
  Flag if the gap between groups grows by more than
  `threshold` across retraining cycles.
  """
  cycles = list(results.values())
  gaps = []
  for cycle in cycles:
    rates = list(cycle.values())
    gaps.append(max(rates) - min(rates))

  drift = gaps[-1] - gaps[0]
  return {
    'initial_gap': round(gaps[0], 4),
    'final_gap': round(gaps[-1], 4),

The Bigger Picture

Feedback loops are what happens when a biased model is treated as a neutral source of truth. The model's predictions become the next generation's labels. The next generation's labels become the next model's signal. The loop closes, and the bias compounds.

This is not a hypothetical. Predictive policing systems trained on over-policed communities have been formally documented producing this exact pattern. Healthcare algorithms that used cost as a proxy for need have been documented under-allocating resources to Black patients — and those allocation decisions then reduced the care-seeking behaviour that would have corrected the signal.

Feedback loops are the reason algorithmic auditing cannot be a one-time exercise at deployment. A model that passes a fairness audit on launch day may fail one a year later — not because anyone changed it, but because it has been changing its own training data the entire time.

Feedback loop bias turns a modest disparity into a severe one, automatically, over time. Auditing once at launch is not enough. Monitor group prediction rates across every retraining cycle — before the loop seals.

WHAT IS DISPARATE TREATMENT?

Disparate impact is accidental discrimination. Disparate treatment is deliberate — and the law treats them very differently.

One-Sentence Definition

Disparate treatment is intentional discrimination: a decision-making process that treats an individual differently because of a protected characteristic — regardless of whether that characteristic is named explicitly or encoded through a feature that serves as a stand-in for it.

Why This Matters

Most algorithmic fairness work focuses on disparate impact — unequal outcomes produced by a model that never explicitly uses a protected attribute. Disparate treatment is the other half of discrimination law, and it's the half that's harder to escape in court.

Where disparate impact asks "did outcomes differ across groups?", disparate treatment asks "was the protected attribute a factor in the decision?" A model that directly includes Gender, Race, or Age as input features is, by definition, engaging in disparate treatment — even if the resulting outcome gap is small. The intent is embedded in the design. Every unfair.py script in this repo is a disparate treatment example before it becomes a disparate impact one.

Disparate Treatment vs. Disparate Impact
Disparate Treatment Disparate Impact
Definition Protected attribute used as a factor in the decision Neutral process produces unequal outcomes across groups
Intent required? Yes — attribute must have been used, by design No — outcome gap is the evidence
Legal standard Title VII §703(a) — treating someone differently because of race, sex, etc. Title VII §703(k) — neutral practice with unjustified disparate effect

Defence BFOQ — narrow, rarely succeeds Business necessity + job-relatedness

  Stage 1: Disparate treatment — protected attrs in feature set?
  Stage 2: Disparate impact — outcome gap > four-fifths threshold?

  passes = ratio >= 0.80
  print(f"DI ratio: {ratio:.3f} — {'✓ passes' if passes else '✗ fails'} four-fifths rule")

The Bigger Picture

Disparate impact and disparate treatment are not alternative explanations for the same thing — they are independent legal claims with independent remedies. A model can fail both, or either one alone. Clearing the treatment audit (no protected attributes in the feature set) is step one of a two-step process. The practical implication: every model that makes decisions about jobs, loans, housing, credit, or benefits needs to pass both audits before deployment. Passing one is not a defence against failing the other. The EEOC's enforcement practice and Title VII case law treat them as two separate gatekeeping steps — and so should your pre-deployment checklist.

Check your feature set for protected attributes before training. Then check your outcome rates for disparate impact after training. Both must clear. Neither alone is enough.

WHAT IS LABEL BIAS?

The training data your model learned from was itself a record of human prejudice. Garbage in, discrimination out — and the model has no idea.

One-Sentence Definition

Label Bias occurs when the ground-truth labels used to train a model reflect past human discrimination rather than actual merit, risk, or outcome — causing the model to learn and perpetuate that discrimination as if it were objective fact.

Why This Matters

Every supervised learning model learns to predict a label. The quiet assumption is that the label is correct. In practice, labels in real-world datasets are often human decisions — hiring outcomes, parole approvals, loan denials, performance ratings — recorded by people operating inside systems that were already discriminatory.

When a model trains on those labels, it doesn't just learn patterns. It learns to reproduce the prejudice baked into every 0 and 1. This is what makes label bias fundamentally different from every other bias type in this series: proxy variables are a problem in the features; label bias is a problem in the target. You cannot detect it by looking at your data pipeline. The pipeline is clean. The labels were just wrong to begin with.

Real-World Proof: Hiring Decisions

A resume-screening model trained on ten years of company hiring data achieves 89% accuracy, passes the Four-Fifths Rule (DI ratio 0.84), and ships. The problem: for those ten years, managers disproportionately rejected women for senior roles due to documented managerial bias. That pattern is now encoded in the label column. The model learned that a woman with 8 years of experience and a high test score is, historically, hired = 0. It is not discriminating against women directly. It is accurately predicting what biased humans would have decided.

How to Detect It in Python

There is no single metric for label bias. Detection requires a combination of approaches. The most reliable: check whether positive label rates differ across protected groups within the same merit tier. A gap that persists — or grows — at the top of the merit distribution is a strong signal that the labels recorded discrimination, not merit.

label_bias_audit.py
import pandas as pd

def label_bias_audit(df, label_col, group_col, feature_cols, n_bins=4):
  """
  Check whether label rates differ across groups within merit-score quantiles.
  If two groups show meaningfully different label rates in the same score band,
  the labels may reflect historical discrimination rather than merit.
  """
  df = df.copy()
  df['_merit'] = df[feature_cols].mean(axis=1)
  df['_bin'] = pd.qcut(df['_merit'], q=n_bins, labels=False, duplicates='drop')

  result = (
    df.groupby(['_bin', group_col])[label_col]
      .mean()
      .unstack(group_col)
      .round(3)
  )
  print("Label rates by merit band and group:")
  print(result)
  print("\nGap that persists or grows at higher merit bands → label bias.")
  return result

def compute_sample_weights(df, label_col, group_col, feature_cols, protected_val):
  """
  Downweight training examples most likely to carry biased labels:
  high-merit, protected-group members who received a negative label.
  These are the records most likely to reflect historical discrimination.
  """
  df = df.copy()
  df['_merit'] = df[feature_cols].mean(axis=1)
  merit_median = df['_merit'].median()

  suspected_biased = (
    (df[group_col] == protected_val) &
    (df['_merit'] > merit_median) &
    (df[label_col] == 0)
  )
  weights = pd.Series(1.0, index=df.index)
  weights[suspected_biased] = 0.5  # downweight suspected biased negatives
  return weights

The Bigger Picture

Label bias forces an uncomfortable question: if the training data records what humans decided, and humans decided in discriminatory ways, is a model trained on that data learning job-relevant patterns — or is it learning to replicate discrimination at scale? The answer is usually both. The model learns real signal and discriminatory signal because both are present in the labels. Standard evaluation cannot distinguish them, because it uses the same labels as the ground truth.

This is why label bias is particularly dangerous in high-stakes domains: criminal justice, hiring, lending, healthcare. These are exactly the domains where historical records exist at scale, where AI adoption is fastest, and where historical discrimination was most systematic — and therefore most thoroughly documented in the label column.

Label bias is also the seed that feedback loops grow from. When a biased model's predictions are harvested as the next generation of training labels, the distortion in the original label column compounds with every retraining cycle. Fixing it at the source matters more than any downstream constraint.

Auditing for label bias requires going beyond the model. It requires asking whether the target variable you trained on was ever a legitimate measure of what you claim to predict — or whether it was always a record of what biased humans decided.

WHAT IS INDIVIDUAL FAIRNESS?

Group fairness says the model treats groups equally. Individual fairness asks something harder: does it treat similar people similarly — regardless of which group they belong to?

One-Sentence Definition

Individual Fairness requires a model to produce similar predictions for individuals who are similar with respect to the task at hand — so that two people who are equally qualified receive equally favourable outcomes, regardless of demographic group membership.

Why This Matters

Every other fairness metric in this series — Demographic Parity, Equalized Odds, Disparate Impact — is a group-level measure. It asks whether two demographic populations receive equal treatment in aggregate. A model can pass every group-level fairness check and still discriminate against a specific individual, so long as that discrimination is consistent enough not to shift the group averages.

Consider two candidates for the same role: identical experience years, identical test score. One is rejected; one is hired. The only meaningful difference is group membership. The Demographic Parity ratio might hover near 0.80 and pass the Four-Fifths Rule. The individual is still harmed. Individual fairness is the standard that catches this — it operates at the level of the person, not the population.

The Formal Definition

Dwork et al. (2012) formalise individual fairness using a task-specific similarity metric d(x, y) over individuals and a distance metric D(f(x), f(y)) over model outputs. The requirement is a Lipschitz condition:

lipschitz_condition.py
# The Lipschitz fairness condition (Dwork et al., 2012)
#
# D(f(x), f(y)) ≤ L · d(x, y)
#
# f(x), f(y) — model predictions for individuals x and y
# d(x, y) — how different x and y are on TASK-RELEVANT features
# D(...) — how different the model outputs are
# L — Lipschitz constant bounding the ratio

# Plain English: if two people are close in the space of features
# that matter for the task, the model's decisions should also be close.
# Demographic group membership should NOT expand the decision gap
# beyond what the task-relevant distance justifies.

# KEY TERM: task-relevant.
# Individual fairness does not require treating everyone identically.
# It requires that outcome differences be proportional to merit
# differences — not inflated by demographic proxies.

Group-level metrics catch the aggregate pattern (4.51pp gender gap). How to Detect It in Python

Three complementary approaches — run all three. They catch different failure modes.

APPROACH 01
Matched-pair test — find identical merit profiles with different outcomes across groups
matched_pair_audit.py
from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import euclidean_distances
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

def individual_fairness_audit(df, prediction_col, group_col, feature_cols, threshold=0.5):
  """
  Find cross-group pairs similar on task features (dist <= threshold)
  but receiving different predictions. These are IF violations.
  """
  df = df.copy().reset_index(drop=True)
  X = StandardScaler().fit_transform(df[feature_cols])
  dists = euclidean_distances(X)
  violations, similar_pairs = 0, 0
  for i in range(len(df)):
    for j in range(i + 1, len(df)):
      if dists[i, j] > threshold: continue
      if df[group_col].iloc[i] == df[group_col].iloc[j]: continue
      similar_pairs += 1
      if df[prediction_col].iloc[i] != df[prediction_col].iloc[j]:
        violations += 1
  rate = violations / similar_pairs if similar_pairs > 0 else None
  print(f"Similar cross-group pairs: {similar_pairs}")
  print(f"Violations: {violations} ({rate*100:.1f}%)")
  # >0% violations = individual fairness problem
  return {"pairs": similar_pairs, "violations": violations, "rate": rate}
APPROACH 02
Lipschitz test — counterfactual group flip: how much does prediction change when only group membership changes?
lipschitz_test.py
import numpy as np

def lipschitz_test(model, X_test, group_col_encoded):
  """Flip the group encoding. Measure how predictions shift.
  High mean delta = group membership is driving the model.
  Near zero = model is robust to group membership changes."""
  X_orig = X_test.copy()
  X_flip = X_test.copy()
  X_flip[group_col_encoded] = 1 - X_flip[group_col_encoded]
  p_orig = model.predict_proba(X_orig)[:, 1]
  p_flip = model.predict_proba(X_flip)[:, 1]
  delta = np.abs(p_orig - p_flip)
  print(f"Mean prediction change: {delta.mean():.4f}")
  print(f"% changed >0.10: {(delta>0.1).mean()*100:.1f}%")
  return delta
APPROACH 03
Consistency score (Zemel et al.) — for each person, how often does the model agree with their k nearest task-feature neighbours?
consistency_score.py
from sklearn.neighbors import NearestNeighbors
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
import numpy as np

def consistency_score(X, y_pred, feature_cols, k=5):
  """1.0 = perfect: same prediction for all similar people.
  Near 0 = different predictions for similar people."""
  X_s = StandardScaler().fit_transform(X[feature_cols])
  _, idx = NearestNeighbors(n_neighbors=k+1).fit(X_s).kneighbors(X_s)
  scores = [
    (y_pred[nbrs[1:]] == y_pred[i]).mean()
    for i, nbrs in enumerate(idx)
  ]
  score = np.mean(scores)
  label = ("Good" if score >= 0.80 else
           "Moderate" if score >= 0.65 else "Poor")
  print(f"Consistency score: {score:.4f} [{label}]")
  return score

The Similarity Metric Problem

Individual fairness has one foundational difficulty: the framework requires a task-specific similarity metric d(x, y), and defining a fair one is not a technical problem — it is a normative one. Who decides that two people are "similar enough" to deserve similar treatment?

If the similarity metric embeds a biased standard — treating tenure_current_employer as task-relevant when it structurally disadvantages career-changers and parents returning from leave — the framework will certify those decisions as consistent. Two people who are equally productive are declared dissimilar. Different outcomes are then permitted by definition.

This is not a flaw to be patched — it is the central normative question individual fairness makes explicit: what does it mean to be equally qualified for this task? That answer requires domain expertise, stakeholder input, and legal grounding. It cannot be solved by optimising a loss function.
The Bigger Picture

Group fairness and individual fairness are not rival frameworks. They are different lenses on the same underlying problem: a decision system that uses demographic signal to produce unequal outcomes for people who deserve equal ones. Group metrics tell you that something is wrong at the population level. Individual fairness tells you exactly what that looks like for a specific person — they submitted the same credentials as someone else and received the opposite verdict.

The combination is also what turns a bias audit into evidence. A Demographic Parity gap tells you there is a problem. Matched-pair violations tell you the mechanism. A defendant flagged high-risk by COMPAS whose criminal history profile is identical to a defendant flagged low-risk is not a statistical artifact — that is a person who can point to a specific instance of unequal treatment.

The goal is not a model that treats groups equally in the aggregate. The goal is a model that gives the same answer to the same person regardless of which group that person belongs to. Group fairness is the aggregate consequence. Individual fairness is the standard.
